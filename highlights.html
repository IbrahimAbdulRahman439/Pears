Highlights of Ghana

From vibrant city life to breathtaking natural landscapes,
Ghana offers a tapestry of experiences waiting to be explored.

Historical Sites

Jamestown Lighhouse

Standing tall in Accra's historic Jamestown, the Jamestown Lighthouse has been a vital guide for ships on the Gulf of Guinea since its beginnings in 1871. The current 28-meter tower, built in the 1930s, reflects Accra's rise as a colonial trading hub. Beyond its function, the lighthouse is an iconic landmark for the Jamestown community, a constant presence amidst change. Its gallery offers sweeping views of the vibrant area, its historic buildings, and the vast Atlantic, linking today's community to its rich maritime heritage. For generations, it has symbolized Accra's enduring connection to the sea.

Cape Coast Castle

Cape Coast Castle, with its stark white walls, tells a tragic story. Built in 1653 by Swedes for trade, the British later expanded it into a central point for the horrific transatlantic slave trade. For centuries, countless Africans were held within its walls before being forced onto ships to the Americas. The "Door of No Return" marks their final step from their homeland. Today, this UNESCO site stands as a powerful museum and memorial, urging reflection on the devastating impact of slavery. Visiting offers a sobering yet vital educational experience.

Independence Square

Independence Square, built in 1961 under Kwame Nkrumah, powerfully symbolizes Ghana's hard-won freedom from British rule in 1957. This expansive ceremonial ground, designed for national pride and celebration, is dominated by the iconic Independence Arch. The Black Star Gate, part of the arch, features the Black Star of Africa, a key symbol of Ghana and Pan-Africanism. Hosting countless national events, the square's vastness and monumental architecture constantly remind Ghanaians of their aspirations and achievements.

Larabanga Mosque

Larabanga Ancient Mosque, in northern Ghana, is a remarkable testament to Islam's long history in West Africa. Founded around 1421, it's among Ghana's oldest mosques, known for its unique Sudanese mud-and-stick architecture and distinct minarets. Local legend says its foundations appeared miraculously after a scholar's dream. Maintained by the community for centuries, it remains an active place of worship and a symbol of the Northern Region's rich cultural heritage, attracting those interested in history and architecture.

Elmina Castle

Elmina Castle, near Cape Coast, shares a similar tragic history in the slave trade. Built by the Portuguese in 1482 for gold, it soon became a major hub for enslaving Africans under various European powers. Like Cape Coast, its dungeons held countless people in horrific conditions before they were forced onto slave ships. Today, this UNESCO site and museum starkly recounts this history, contrasting the dungeons with the governor's quarters, offering a powerful and essential lesson on the impact of slavery.

Kwame Nkrumah Mausoleum

This striking memorial park honors Kwame Nkrumah, Ghana's first president and a key figure in African independence. Completed in the early 1990s, its mausoleum, shaped like an overturned Ghanaian stool (a symbol of authority), holds Nkrumah and his wife. The park's museum details Nkrumah's life, political journey, and Pan-African vision through artifacts, photos, and documents, making it a center for learning and remembrance of his crucial role in Ghana and African liberation.

Salaga Slave Market

Located in northern Ghana, the Salaga Slave Market was a major 18th and 19th-century hub for the internal slave trade and trans-Saharan routes, also trading goods like kola nuts and gold. Enslaved people endured harsh conditions, with sites like the "Wonkan Bawa" pond and a baobab tree serving as grim reminders. The British influence led to its decline, effectively ending by 1896. Today, the site is being redeveloped for remembrance and education.

The Asante Traditional Buildings

Near Kumasi, whispers of the mighty Asante Kingdom (1701-1901) echo in its last traditional buildings. These mud-walled shrines were once vibrant spiritual hubs. Ancient stories are etched in bold carvings – symbolic "Adinkra" patterns holding generations of wisdom. Though weathered, these resilient structures reveal the architectural ingenuity and rich culture of a powerful West African empire. They stand as captivating storytellers of a golden age.

Natural Attractions

Kakum National Park

Kakum National Park's story began as a protected forest in 1931, valued for its water resources. Logging was a threat until 1992, when local efforts led to its designation as a National Park, unique in its grassroots origin. Its key attraction, the breathtaking canopy walkway opened in 1995, offers unique rainforest views. Today, Kakum is a vital conservation area, showcasing diverse wildlife and plant life, making it a popular ecotourism destination. Its history is intertwined with its iconic canopy and rich biodiversity.

Mole National Park

Mole National Park, Ghana's largest wildlife refuge, was established as a game reserve in 1958 and became a National Park in 1971. Its key attraction is its abundant savanna wildlife, including elephants, antelopes, buffalo, and diverse birdlife. Visitors can experience this landscape through walking and driving safaris. Historically encompassing hunting grounds and slave trade routes, Mole is now a vital conservation area and a significant ecotourism destination, its history linked to its vast savanna and rich wildlife.

Aburi Botanical Gardens

Established in 1890 by the British, initially as a research station to test the viability of various crops like cocoa and rubber. Over time, it evolved into a public garden, showcasing a diverse collection of indigenous and exotic flora, becoming a serene escape with historical significance.

Mount Afadjato

For generations, this peak has held cultural significance for the local Ewe people, with the name meaning "at war with the bush," reflecting local beliefs. While not having a singular "establishment" date, its history is deeply intertwined with the traditions and legends of the surrounding communities. It gained prominence as a hiking destination with increasing tourism in Ghana.

Shai Hills Resource Reserve

Declared a forest reserve in 1962, it was later upgraded to a resource reserve to protect its wildlife and unique inselberg landscape. The area has historical significance, once inhabited by the Shai people before their relocation in the late 19th century.

Nzulezu Village

The origins of this stilt village on Lake Tadane are rooted in the traditions of the Nzema people, who are believed to have migrated from the Walata region in the ancient Ghana Empire. Oral history suggests the lake offered refuge and a unique way of life, with the village existing for centuries, adapting to its watery environment.

Wechiau Community Hippo Sanctuary

Established through the efforts of local communities in the late 1990s to protect the endangered hippos and their habitat along the Black Volta River. This community-led initiative marked a shift towards sustainable conservation practices, recognizing the ecological and potential economic value of the wildlife.

Paga Crocodile Pond

The sacred nature of the crocodiles dates back centuries, linked to local legends of a crocodile saving a lost ancestor. The pond's history is deeply embedded in the traditions and spiritual beliefs of the people of Paga, with strict rules governing interaction with the reptiles.

Key Water Bodies

Volta River

The largest river system in Ghana, formed by the merging of the Black and White Volta. Its name, given by the Portuguese in the 15th century, means "turn" due to its winding course. The river was significantly altered by the construction of the Akosombo Dam in the 1960s, creating Lake Volta, one of the world's largest artificial lakes, and providing crucial hydroelectric power.

Black Volta River

Rising in the highlands of Burkina Faso, this river flows for approximately 1,352 kilometers before joining the White Volta in Ghana to form the main Volta River. It forms parts of the borders between Ghana and both Burkina Faso and Côte d'Ivoire. The Bui Dam, another significant hydroelectric project, is located on the Black Volta.

White Volta River

Originating north of Ouagadougou in Burkina Faso, the White Volta (also known as Nakambé) flows southward for about 885 kilometers through northern Ghana until it meets the Black Volta. It's a major source of water for many communities and experiences seasonal flooding. The construction of the Bagré Dam in Burkina Faso in 1992 has influenced its water levels.

Ankobra River

Located in southwestern Ghana, this river flows for about 190 kilometers from northeast of Wiawso south to the Gulf of Guinea. Small ships can navigate its lower reaches. Historically, it was used to transport machinery to gold mining areas, and the mouth of the river is near the remains of a former Dutch trading post, Fort Elize Carthago, abandoned in 1711.

Lake Volta

The world's largest artificial lake by surface area, created by the Akosombo Dam in the mid-1960s. This vast body of water is crucial for generating a significant portion of Ghana's electricity and also serves as a vital waterway for transportation and a major fishing ground.

Lake Bosomtwi

Ghana's only natural inland lake, formed by a meteorite impact about a million years ago. Located near Kumasi, it holds deep cultural significance for the Ashanti people and is a popular spot for recreation and relaxation. Local traditions consider it a sacred lake.

Boti Falls

A captivating twin waterfall located in the Eastern Region. The two cascading streams are referred to as male and female, and local myth says a rainbow forms when they merge, symbolizing a mating ceremony. The falls were reportedly discovered by a white Catholic priest, and Ghana's first president, Kwame Nkrumah, helped popularize them.

Wli Waterfalls

The highest waterfall in Ghana and West Africa, located in the Volta Region near the Togo border. Known locally as 'Agoomatsa' (meaning "Allow Me to Flow"), it has both a lower and upper fall, with the hike to the upper falls being more challenging. The surrounding Agumatsa Wildlife Sanctuary is home to diverse wildlife, including a large colony of fruit bats.
