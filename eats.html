Ghanaian
Culinary Delights

Welcome to a culinary journey through the vibrant
flavors and rich traditions of Ghanaian cuisine.

Staple Dishes

Fufu and Soup

No exploration of Ghanaian food is complete without experiencing Fufu and Soup. This beloved dish features a smooth, soft dough made from pounded cassava and plantains (or yam/cocoyam), lovingly paired with a rich and flavorful soup. From the comforting spice of Light Soup to the nutty depth of Groundnut Soup and the distinctly Ashanti favorite, Palm Nut Soup (Abenkwan), each spoonful offers a true taste of Ghanaian tradition and culinary heart. Eaten with your hands, Fufu and Soup is more than a meal ‚Äì it's a cultural experience.

Banku and Tilapia

Banku, a smooth and slightly tangy dough made from fermented corn and cassava, is a popular staple across Ghana. Often served alongside perfectly grilled or fried Tilapia, this combination offers a delightful contrast in textures and flavors. The dish is typically enhanced with a fiery and flavorful pepper sauce, known as "shito," creating a satisfying and distinctly Ghanaian culinary experience enjoyed for lunch or dinner.

Kenkey and Fried Fish

Kenkey is a unique and flavorful staple made from fermented corn dough, traditionally wrapped in corn husks or plantain leaves and steamed. This gives it a slightly tangy taste and a firm texture. It's commonly served with crispy fried fish and a spicy, flavorful pepper sauce. Often enjoyed as a satisfying meal on its own, Kenkey and Fish is a testament to the ingenuity of Ghanaian culinary traditions.

Waakye

Waakye is a delicious and uniquely Ghanaian dish where rice and black-eyed peas are cooked together with special leaves that impart a distinctive color and subtle flavor. This hearty staple is often served with a variety of tasty accompaniments, such as spaghetti, fried plantain (k…îk…î…î), boiled eggs, avocado, and a spicy stew or pepper sauce. A popular choice for a satisfying breakfast or lunch across Ghana, including right here in the Ashanti Region.

Tuo Zaafi

Tuo Zaafi, often simply called TZ, is a smooth, thick porridge-like staple made from millet or corn flour, particularly popular in the northern regions of Ghana but enjoyed across the country. It's traditionally served with flavorful and often nutritious soups like groundnut soup or the distinctive Ayoyo soup (jute leaves soup), creating a hearty and satisfying meal with a unique texture and taste. Eating TZ with soup is a cherished part of Ghanaian culinary culture.

Red Red

Red Red is a hearty and visually appealing Ghanaian dish made from black-eyed peas cooked in red palm oil, which gives it its signature reddish hue. This flavorful stew is often served with ripe fried plantains, creating a satisfying and delicious combination of savory and sweet. It's a popular and comforting meal enjoyed throughout Ghana.

Omo Tuo

Omo Tuo, "rice balls" in Twi, is a simple yet cherished Ghanaian staple, especially in the Ashanti Region. Soft rice, boiled with extra water, is pounded smooth and shaped into balls. Its mild taste makes it ideal for soaking up rich Ghanaian soups. Popular pairings include groundnut, palm nut, and light soup. The soft texture enhances enjoyment with the flavorful broth. It highlights rice's versatility in Ghanaian cuisine.

Gari Foto

Gari Foto is a simple and popular Ghanaian dish where cooked gari (granulated cassava) is mixed directly with a flavorful and saucy stew or beans. The granular gari softens as it absorbs the liquid, taking on the savory taste of the stew or beans. Prepared by simply stirring the gari into the hot stew or beans until the desired consistency is reached, Gari Foto is often enjoyed hot for lunch or a light dinner, sometimes with additions like fried eggs or sardines, showcasing a resourceful and convenient way to create a satisfying meal from readily available ingredients.

Rice Dishes

Jollof Rice

Jollof Rice is a vibrant and incredibly popular rice dish enjoyed throughout West Africa, and Ghana's version is fiercely loved! Cooked in a rich and flavorful tomato-based stew with onions, peppers, and various spices, Jollof Rice achieves a beautiful reddish-orange hue and a deeply satisfying savory taste. Often served with grilled or fried chicken, fish, or a side salad, it's a staple at celebrations and a must-try for anyone exploring Ghanaian cuisine. The aroma alone is irresistible!

Fried Rice

While a popular dish worldwide, Ghanaian Fried Rice often incorporates local flavors and ingredients. Typically featuring stir-fried rice with mixed vegetables (like carrots, peas, and green beans), it commonly includes scrambled eggs, chicken, shrimp, or sausage. You'll often find it seasoned with local spices and sometimes a hint of ginger, making it a satisfying and adaptable meal option found in many Ghanaian eateries.

Agwa Mu

Agwa Mu, also known simply as "oiled rice," is a delicious and easy-to-prepare Ghanaian rice dish cooked with regular cooking oil (like vegetable oil) that has been infused with the flavor of saut√©ed onions and tomatoes. It often includes smoked fish, dried shrimp, or other seasonings for added depth. This dish offers a comforting savory taste and is a popular choice for a quick and satisfying meal.

Plain Rice with Stew

Simple boiled long-grain rice is a fundamental and versatile part of Ghanaian cuisine. Its mild flavor and fluffy texture make it the perfect accompaniment to the wide array of flavorful stews (such as tomato stew, fish stew, or vegetable stew) and rich soups (like groundnut soup or light soup) that are central to Ghanaian meals. Don't forget the essential side of "shito" (black pepper sauce) to add a spicy kick and complete the experience!

Soup & Stews

Groundnut Soup

A rich and creamy Ghanaian soup, Groundnut Soup is made with groundnuts (peanuts) as its base, often featuring chicken or other meats, and various vegetables for added flavor and texture. The groundnuts are typically blended into a smooth paste, which is then simmered with the other ingredients. This hearty and flavorful soup is a popular accompaniment to Fufu, rice, or Banku.

Palm Nut Soup
(Abenkwan)

Palm Nut Soup, or Abenkwan, is a flavorful Ghanaian soup made from the extract of palm fruit. The palm fruit is boiled to soften it, then pounded to extract its pulp, which is simmered with fish or meat and vegetables. This rich and savory soup is a staple in Ghanaian cuisine and is often served with Fufu or Banku.

Okro Soup
(Nkrumahkwan)

Okro Soup, also known as Nkrumahkwan, is a distinctive Ghanaian soup characterized by its slightly slimy texture, derived from the okra (okro) used as a key ingredient. The soup typically includes tomatoes, onions, and often fish or crab for added flavor. It's a popular and flavorful accompaniment to Banku or Fufu.

Kontomire

Kontomire Stew is a nutritious Ghanaian stew made primarily from cocoyam leaves (also known as kontomire), tomatoes, and onions. It often includes smoked fish or other protein sources and sometimes eggs. This flavorful and wholesome stew is commonly eaten with rice, yam, or plantain.

Snacks & Street Food

Kelewele

Kelewele is a popular and irresistible Ghanaian snack made from ripe plantains that are seasoned with a blend of spices, including ginger, cayenne pepper, and sometimes cloves or nutmeg, before being deep-fried until golden brown and slightly caramelized. The result is a delightful combination of sweet and spicy flavors that makes it a perfect anytime treat.

Kofi Brokeman

Kofi Brokeman is a classic Ghanaian snack featuring roasted plantains served with roasted groundnuts (peanuts). The sweetness of the plantain pairs perfectly with the earthy, nutty flavor of the groundnuts, creating a simple yet incredibly satisfying and energy-boosting snack that's widely enjoyed.

Tatale

Tatale are delicious Ghanaian pancakes or fritters made from overripe plantains that are mashed and mixed with flour, spices (like nutmeg and ginger), and sometimes a touch of sugar or salt. These are then pan-fried until golden brown. Tatale can be enjoyed as a sweet snack or a savory side dish with beans or other meals.

Bofrot

Bofrot are the beloved Ghanaian version of doughnuts. These small, golden-brown balls of dough are deep-fried until light and fluffy on the inside and slightly crispy on the outside. Often dusted with sugar, they make a perfect sweet treat to enjoy with a drink or as a simple indulgence.

Roasted Corn

A simple yet satisfying Ghanaian snack, roasted corn features fresh ears grilled over hot coals to achieve tender, slightly charred kernels with a unique smoky flavor. Typically roasted in the husk, sometimes with a touch of butter or salt, it's a popular street food enjoyed right off the cob, often plain or with a sprinkle of seasoning, especially during corn season.

Chinchinga

Chichinga is a spicy and flavorful Ghanaian kebab made from small pieces of marinated meat (usually beef, chicken, or mutton) skewered and grilled over an open flame until cooked through and slightly charred. The marinade includes spices similar to suya. This popular street food is often sold hot by vendors and served with sliced onions and tomatoes as a tasty and convenient snack.

Fish Balls

A popular snack, especially in Ghana's coastal regions, fish balls are savory, deep-fried spheres made from ground fish paste seasoned with onions, peppers, and various spices, sometimes with a binding agent. Shaped into small balls and fried until golden brown and crispy, they are frequently sold as a quick snack or appetizer, enjoyed plain or with a dipping sauce, and can also be part of a larger meal.

Koose
(Bean Fritters)

Koose, also known as Kose or Akara, are savory fritters made from soaked and ground black-eyed peas seasoned with onions, peppers, and spices, then deep-fried into golden brown and crispy balls or patties. A beloved breakfast item often paired with Hausa Koko, Koose is also enjoyed as a snack throughout the day and is commonly sold by street vendors across Ghana.

Drinks

Bissap (Sobolo)

Bissap, widely known as Sobolo in Ghana, is a vibrant and refreshing drink made from steeped hibiscus flowers. This popular beverage boasts a beautiful deep red color and a naturally tart flavor that's often balanced with sugar and infused with spices like ginger, cloves, and sometimes local fruits. It's a favorite thirst quencher enjoyed throughout Accra, readily available from street vendors to restaurants.

Ginger Drink

Homemade Ginger Drink is a popular and invigorating beverage in Ghana, especially in Accra. Made by boiling fresh ginger with water and often sweetened with sugar, it offers a naturally spicy and refreshing kick. Some variations may include other spices like cloves or a touch of lemon or pineapple for added flavor. It's enjoyed on its own or as a flavorful accompaniment to meals.

Palm Wine

Palm Wine is a traditional alcoholic beverage tapped from various types of palm trees. Freshly tapped palm wine is sweet, but it naturally ferments quickly, developing a more complex and slightly tangy flavor with an alcoholic content. It's a cultural drink often enjoyed in social gatherings and can be found in various parts of Ghana, including some local spots around Accra, offering a unique taste of Ghanaian tradition.

Pito

Pito is a traditional Ghanaian alcoholic beverage brewed from fermented millet or sorghum. While particularly popular in the northern regions, you might find it in some local settings in Accra. It has a characteristically slightly sour and earthy taste and is often enjoyed in social gatherings.
